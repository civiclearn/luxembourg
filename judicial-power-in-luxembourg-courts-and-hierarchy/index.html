The judicial power in Luxembourg

In Luxembourg, the judicial power is one of the three fundamental powers of the state, alongside the legislative and executive powers. It is responsible for applying the law and resolving disputes in an independent manner.

For Vivre-Ensemble candidates, it is important to understand that judges act independently and are not subject to instructions from the government.

Independence of the courts

Judicial independence is a key principle in Luxembourg. Courts and judges must be able to decide cases based solely on the law, without political pressure or interference.

This principle guarantees fairness, equality before the law and respect for fundamental rights.

Main categories of courts

The Luxembourg judicial system includes several types of courts. Ordinary courts deal mainly with civil and criminal matters, while administrative courts handle disputes involving public authorities.

This distinction allows cases to be examined by judges specialised in the relevant field of law.

Hierarchy of courts

Courts in Luxembourg are organised in a hierarchy. Lower courts examine cases first, while higher courts review decisions in case of appeal.

This hierarchical structure ensures legal certainty and allows errors to be corrected through the appeals process.

The Constitutional Court

The Constitutional Court has a specific role. It examines whether laws comply with the Constitution when a constitutional question is raised during a judicial procedure.

This mechanism reinforces the protection of constitutional principles in Luxembourg.

Why this topic matters for the Vivre-Ensemble test

Test questions often focus on identifying the judicial power, understanding the independence of judges and recognising the role of different courts.

Candidates should be able to distinguish the judicial power from the legislative and executive powers.
