The Treaty of London (1867) and Luxembourg’s Neutrality

The agreement that secured Luxembourg’s independence and permanent neutrality.

Luxembourg in the 19th century

During the 19th century, Luxembourg found itself at the center of tensions between major European powers.

Its strategic location and fortified capital made it a subject of international concern.

The Luxembourg Crisis

In the 1860s, a diplomatic crisis emerged when the future of Luxembourg was contested by neighboring powers.

The situation risked triggering a broader European conflict.

The Treaty of London of 1867

To resolve the crisis, European powers met in London and signed a treaty in 1867.

The treaty recognized Luxembourg as an independent state and guaranteed its permanent neutrality.

Demilitarization of the fortress

One of the treaty’s key provisions was the dismantling of Luxembourg City’s massive fortifications.

This transformed the city and marked the end of its role as a major military fortress.

Neutrality as a guiding principle

The Treaty of London established neutrality as a fundamental element of Luxembourg’s foreign policy.

This status shaped the country’s behavior during later European conflicts.
